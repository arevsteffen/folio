Leading Design Without a Title

Most design leadership conversations start with the org chart. Who reports to whom, what level you're at, whether you have a seat at the table. These things matter — but they're downstream of something more fundamental: whether people actually trust your judgment.

I've worked with senior designers who had every formal authority and couldn't move a product decision. I've worked with ICs with no direct reports who shaped entire product directions. The difference wasn't the title. It was how they showed up in rooms.

Influence before authority

The designers who lead effectively — with or without a formal leadership role — tend to share a few habits. They make their thinking legible. They don't just show work; they show why. They frame problems before proposing solutions. They ask the question no one else is asking, and they ask it early.

This kind of influence is slower to build than positional authority. It requires consistency over time — being the person who follows through, who does the unglamorous research, who synthesizes feedback without getting defensive. But it also compounds in ways that titles don't.

Craft as credibility

There's a version of design leadership that drifts entirely into strategy and facilitation — away from the actual pixels and flows. Sometimes that's right. But I've found that maintaining a connection to the craft, even at a senior level, keeps you credible in a particular way that nothing else does.

When you can sit down and show the work — not just talk about it — people take your opinions on process and direction more seriously. Craft isn't separate from leadership. For designers, it's the foundation of it.
